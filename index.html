Systems Across Time

From ancient philosophy and Roman civic culture to smart cities, education access, wellbeing, sustainability, economics, and writing, this site traces how systems shape the way people live together.

Begin with ancient systems Jump to modern systems

Guiding Question

What do different eras reveal about the rules beneath society?

The activities in this collection are grouped by the kind of system they explore. Some look backward toward ancient schools of thought and Roman culture. Others examine the modern systems that are often invisible until someone studies, designs, organizes, or writes about them.

Part I

Systems of Ancient Times

These projects focus on older systems of knowledge, culture, education, and civic imagination.

Thought Systems 01

Research in Comparative Philosophy

Participated in a research trip to New College, Oxford to compare schools of thought from ancient times.

System
How civilizations organize ethics, knowledge, and ways of reasoning.
Role
Student researcher studying comparisons across ancient intellectual traditions.
Cultural Systems 02

Latin Club

Created a student club focused on Roman history and culture, with help from younger students.

System
How language, memory, and institutions preserve ancient civic life.
Impact
Built a learning community with 15+ members across grade levels.

Part II

Invisible Modern Systems

These activities reveal the systems underneath cities, schools, mental health, climate action, economics, and public storytelling.

Urban Systems 03

Smart Cities and IEEE Young Engineers Conference

Researched urban design at Teachers College, Columbia University and presented work on apps that could improve walking in cities through specific UX changes and behavior data.

System
How digital tools, streets, and human behavior interact in the built environment.
Impact
Received a merit award at the conference.
Education Systems 04

Closing Reflection

The same question keeps returning in different forms.

Whether studying ancient philosophy, building a Latin club, researching walkable cities, organizing education access, supporting wellbeing, advancing sustainability, writing economics essays, or crafting stories, each project asks how individual action can make a larger system more visible, more humane, and more thoughtfully designed.
